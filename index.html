Protecting the hands that lift, hold & catch — at 15 mph on ice

Development of Ultra-Thin, High-Impact Protective Wearables for Hand and Finger Safety in Synchronized Figure Skating

No protective gear exists for hands and fingers in synchro skating — despite blade run-over injuries causing lacerations, crushed bones, and tendon damage. This research project aims to close the safety gap.

The invisible risk on ice

Synchronized skating teams perform at 12–15 mph in tight formations. When a skater falls, following skaters cannot stop in time — their blades run over the fallen skater's exposed hands.

Consequences: lacerations, crushed bones, severed tendons, career-ending injuries

  • Hockey gloves → too bulky, restrict hand-holding and grip
  • Standard gloves → zero impact or cut protection against blades
  • General padding → not designed for ice impact + blade cut risk + dexterity needs

Research objectives

1. Material discovery

Identify ultra-thin materials with high impact absorption and cut resistance while maintaining flexibility for complex hand movements.

2. Wearable design

Design a lightweight, low-profile prototype (glove or finger sleeves) that fits inside standard skating gloves and permits full range of motion.

3. Testing & validation

Rigorous testing for impact reduction, cut resistance, dexterity, comfort, and durability — validated with active synchronized skaters.

Expected outcomes

Functional Prototype

A wearable hand protection system designed specifically for synchronized skating — combining cut-proof layers and impact-dispersing materials at just 1–2mm thickness.

Impact reduction ≥40% Blade cut resistance (ANSI/ISO level) Full finger articulation

Why this matters

For Skaters

Prevents career-ending hand injuries, enables confident training, and allows athletes to push performance without fear.

For the Sport

Addresses a critical safety gap as synchronized skating grows globally (Olympic momentum). Safer sport = longer careers.

For Materials Science

Advances ultra-thin protective materials with broader applications: industrial gloves, military, high-dexterity robotics, and medical devices.
